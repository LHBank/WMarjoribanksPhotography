Mingalaba, MyanmarBlog Post Heading

Throughout my Antipodean/Asia-expedition tenure, I’ve been fortunate to have met some of my closest friends. They’re not nearby in a physical sense, but with the development of video-calling, they’re only a time zone away, sometimes several. One of my closest friends from the Philippines, told me about a city called Bacolod, which is often referred to as, “The City of Smiles”. I grew up in Scotland and despite there being a plethora of Munro’s to scale and midges (think smaller, less-malaria prone mosquito equivalent) to swear at, as of 2019 a third of our population is recorded to be obese. I’d much rather it had the reputation of being the ‘Home of Unicorns’ or ‘Place you’re most likely to be bought a drink, by a not-so-tight Scotsman’. Alas, we’re neither.

Well, Myanmar – similar to Bacolod, was truly one of the warmest countries, both climate and friendliness factor, I’ve explored. Everyone, and I mean, almost everyone, you pass in the street, be it downtown Yangon or the sandy country lanes of Bagan, will smile the greeting ‘Mingalaba’ in your direction. And if they don’t say it first, then it’s your turn – they will respond.

Most of the general population, men and women adorn national dress. The Longyi. It’s a tubular piece of cotton which replaces the need for trousers – and growing up in Scotland, the home of the Kilt – I jumped at the opportunity to wear a skirt in 35-degree heat. I attracted some puzzled facial expressions and (jealous) remarks, when I wore it around the house I share in Dublin. It’s the most comfortable piece of clothing I own. Like the pyjama pants westerners will likely invest in, which inherit no stigma, despite resembling MC-Hammer. Double standard much. After two days of walking the busy, betel-quid spattered pavements of Yangon, I invested in my Longyi and brought it with me everywhere. This meant I wasn’t disrespecting the conservative rule when I wanted to enter a Pagoda or temple in Old Bagan, of which there are 3500 still remaining in from the 13th century.

These architectural marvels are dispersed over forty square miles of sand-dune countryside, and if you don’t own a map, then your chances of missing hot-air balloons floating inches above the rooftops at sunrise, is high. Unless of course, somebody local, sees the bleary eyes of a tourist eScooter mob, and takes pity. Our Samaritan trundled over to us, and asked us where we wanted to go, at 4:30am. We specified a temple rooftop, and he almost scoffed. In unbroken English, he said, “Follow me, that’s not where you need to go”. So, willingly, we followed a complete stranger, to what can only be described as the quintessential sunrise, you could ever hope to feast your eyes upon. The peach glow as the backdrop, for which a blinding golden-tiled Pagoda rooftop sat in front. The five-strong mob and I sat in awe until 6:30am. Our guide, soon departed, and as a token of our appreciation wanted to give him something. He wouldn’t accept anything of monetary value, and instead responded, “I am proud to show you my country, I don’t need money”. In true Burmese fashion, we all exchanged smiles, and I a handshake, and we scootered off. I think that’s what a lot of people have forgotten. Acknowledging and enjoying the aspects of life, we take for granted. I for one am certainly guilty of this.

So if there were any way to wrap this up, it would be to echo the twelfth rule of 'Twelves Rules to Life: An Antidote to Chaos' by Jordan B. Peterson, is the easiest and most relevant to practice - "Pet a cat when you encounter one on the street".
